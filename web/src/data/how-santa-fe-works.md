# How Santa Fe Functions

A contextual guide to the City of Santa Fe's governance, finances, and operations
-- written to accompany the FY 2025-26 budget data surfaced in this portal.

---

## Santa Fe at a Glance

Santa Fe is the capital of New Mexico and the oldest state capital in the United
States, founded in 1610. Population is approximately 87,500 (2020 census). At
roughly 7,000 feet of elevation in the Sangre de Cristo foothills, it is also
the highest-elevation state capital in the country.

The city occupies a dual role: it is a **home-rule municipality** governing its
own affairs under a charter adopted in 1988, and simultaneously the **seat of
state government**, hosting the Legislature, the Governor's Office, and the
state Supreme Court. The city has no administrative authority over these state
functions, but their presence shapes the local economy, land use, and traffic
patterns.

**Key facts:**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Founded | 1610 |
| Population | ~87,500 (2020) |
| Elevation | ~7,000 ft |
| Area | ~37 square miles |
| UNESCO designation | Creative City (2005, first U.S. city inducted) |
| Art galleries | 250+ |
| Arts/culture employment | ~10% of total employment |
| Median home listing price | ~$670,000 (2025) |
| Median household income | ~$78,000 |
| FY26 all-funds budget | $480.6 million |

Santa Fe's economy is anchored by **tourism, government, and the arts**. The
adopted FY26 budget projects $18 million in lodgers' tax revenue alone. But the
same forces that make the city attractive -- historic architecture, natural
beauty, a vibrant cultural scene -- have also fueled a **severe housing
affordability crisis**. The median listing price is roughly 8.5 times the
median household income, putting homeownership out of reach for most residents
who work in the city.

## Governance Structure

Santa Fe operates under a **home-rule charter** with a structure often described
as a modified strong-mayor / council-manager hybrid.

### Executive Branch

- **Mayor**: Elected citywide to a 4-year term. Serves as chief executive and
  presides over governing body meetings.
- **City Manager**: Appointed by the governing body. Serves as chief
  administrative officer responsible for day-to-day operations.

### Legislative Branch

- **Governing Body**: An 8-member City Council elected from four single-member
  districts, each with two councilors serving staggered 4-year terms. Formally
  called the "governing body" in the charter.
- **Finance Committee**: A standing committee of the governing body that reviews
  the annual budget before it goes to the full council. Former City Manager Mark
  Scott advocated for presenting the proposed budget to the entire governing
  body rather than only the Finance Committee.

### Distinctive Governance Features

Santa Fe's charter includes several features that are uncommon among U.S. cities
of its size:

- **Ranked-choice voting** in municipal elections (second cycle completed in the
  March 2025 election)
- **Public campaign financing** for local races
- **Direct democracy provisions**: referendum, initiative, recall, and a formal
  citizen complaint process
- **Policy statements** embedded in the charter on ethics, environmental
  protection, cultural preservation, and support for local business

## Revenue: The "One-Trick Pony"

In his December 2025 departing memo, former City Manager Mark Scott described
Santa Fe as a "one-trick pony" when it comes to revenue. That trick is the
**Gross Receipts Tax (GRT)**, New Mexico's equivalent of a sales tax.

### GRT Dominance

GRT is the largest contributor to the general fund and to the city's overall tax
revenue. In the adopted FY26 budget:

| Tax Source | FY26 Amount | Share of Tax Revenue |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Gross Receipts Tax | $167.4M | 80.8% |
| Lodgers' Tax | $18.0M | 8.7% |
| Property Tax | $13.2M | 6.4% |
| Franchise Fees | $5.6M | 2.7% |
| Gasoline Tax | $1.7M | 0.8% |
| Other Taxes | $1.3M | 0.6% |
| **Total** | **$207.2M** | **100%** |

Santa Fe's combined GRT rate is **8.25%** (state 5.125% + local 3.125%). Under
state law, municipalities may impose a municipal gross receipts tax (MGRT) up
to 2.5% through a combination of ordinance (up to 2.05%) and voter-approved
increments (up to 0.45%). Scott argued "strong consideration should be made to
increase the City's GRT rate" because "it is far below the max."

### Volatility

GRT revenue has nearly doubled over five years -- from roughly $113M in FY21 to
$167M budgeted for FY26 -- largely driven by the implementation of the internet
sales tax. But this growth masks real volatility. GRT revenues dropped sharply
during COVID-19, and because the tax is tied to economic activity (tourism,
retail, construction), any downturn hits the city's operating budget directly.

The Finance Committee has emphasized "the need for a more diverse portfolio of
revenue sources to ensure financial stability."

### Other Revenue Themes

- **Fees and charges**: Scott recommended a comprehensive review of whether
  city fees actually cover the cost of providing services, or whether the city
  is implicitly subsidizing specific activities.
- **Developer impact fees**: The city maintains an Impact Fee Capital
  Improvements Plan (currently covering roads, parks, fire/EMS, and police).
  Scott called for renewed scrutiny of these fees.
- **Affordable Housing Trust Fund**: Established by city ordinance in 2007. Now
  funded primarily by a 3% excise tax on home sales exceeding $1 million
  (upheld by the NM Court of Appeals in 2025). The fund requires a 3:1 leverage
  ratio -- every public dollar must attract $3 in private investment. It has
  generated over $27 million in total affordable housing investment impacting
  more than 2,500 households.
- **Interest income**: Investment returns have become meaningful -- $15.3M
  budgeted for FY26, up from essentially zero in FY22.

## The Budget System: What the Numbers Don't Show

The FY26 budget presents clean numbers: $480.6 million in expenditures across
20 departments, $482.7 million in revenue, a General Fund of $161 million. But
the infrastructure behind those numbers is less tidy.

### How the Budget Gets Made

Scott described the city's budget system as "abysmal" -- a characterization he
said "everyone knows." The budget development process historically relied on
**homemade Excel spreadsheets**. The city began implementing **Questica**
(now Euna Solutions) budget software, but Scott cautioned that "the software to
do the budget numbers is way behind" and that Questica "is just a tool. It is
not a solution to good budgeting."

In his final months, Scott initiated a framework of **goals, objectives, and key
performance indicators (KPIs)** as the foundation for a performance-based
budget process. That work was still nascent at the time of his departure.

### What's In the Budget

The adopted FY26 budget covers all city operating funds:

- **20 departments** from Public Utilities ($100.7M, the largest) down to the
  newly created Santa Fe Film Office ($505K)
- **Five expenditure categories**: Personnel Services ($170.3M), Operating
  Expenses ($193.2M), Capital Outlay ($14.2M), Debt Service ($22.6M), and
  Transfers Out ($80.3M)
- **General Fund** totals $161 million, with Police ($36.8M), Fire ($34.7M),
  and Public Works ($30.2M) as the three largest departments
- **Revenue** of $482.7M across taxes, fees, transfers, grants, investment
  income, and other sources

### What's NOT In the Budget

Certain entities that affect Santa Fe's full financial picture are **excluded**
from the adopted budget totals:

- **Buckman Direct Diversion (BDD)**: A jointly operated water diversion
  facility (City of Santa Fe, Santa Fe County, and Las Campanas) that provides
  roughly 50% of the city's water supply by conveying Colorado River surface
  water through tunnels under the Continental Divide. The city's share is
  approximately $7.2M. The BDD has its own board and budget process.
- **Santa Fe Solid Waste Management Agency**: Operates separately from city
  funds despite the city budgeting $16.6M in solid waste fees.

### Open Questions

Two structural issues flagged by Scott remain unresolved:

1. **Interfund transfers**: The FY26 budget includes $80.3 million in Transfers
   Out -- the second-largest expenditure category. Scott recommended bringing in
   a consultant to "evaluate how the City does its Interfund Transfers so that
   clear policies can be established and so that third-party oversight is
   possible." Without transparent transfer policies, it is difficult to trace
   how money actually flows between funds.

2. **Available General Fund balance**: Scott and Finance Director Emily Oster
   were working to answer the question "How much of the City's General Fund
   balance is truly available for allocation?" Scott noted that the answer is
   "NOT simple" and could fill pages of explanation. The distinction matters
   because committed, restricted, or encumbered balances may appear as
   available on paper while being functionally locked up.

## Key Financial Highlights

Items worth understanding when viewing the budget data:

### Revenue Concentration Risk

Four-fifths of the city's tax revenue comes from a single source. The revenue
vs. expenditure trend (FY22 through FY26) shows the gap narrowing:

| Year | Revenue | Expenditure | Surplus |
|------|---------|-------------|---------|
| FY 21/22 | $377.8M | $330.8M | $47.0M |
| FY 22/23 | $408.4M | $347.3M | $61.1M |
| FY 23/24 | $496.5M | $447.9M | $48.6M |
| FY 24/25 | $448.9M | $440.1M | $8.8M |
| FY 25/26 | $482.7M | $480.6M | $2.1M |

The implied surplus has shrunk from $47M to $2M over four years. If GRT revenue
underperforms projections in any given year, the city has very little cushion.

### Personnel Costs

Personnel Services is the fastest-growing major category: $107M in FY22 to
$170M budgeted for FY26 (59% growth in four years). The FY26 budget includes:

- 3% across-the-board raises for all city employees
- Final two phases of a multi-year compensation restructuring
- $633,900 in additional Parks and Open Space funding for contract crews

Salaries and wages alone account for $114.8M; benefits add another $55.5M.
Together, personnel costs represent 35% of all-funds spending.

### Debt and Bond Ratings

The city's credit profile is strong but recently shifted:

| Obligation | S&P Rating | Fitch Rating |
|------------|-----------|--------------|
| General Obligation Bonds | AA | AA |
| Water Utility Revenue Bonds | AAA | AA+ |
| Wastewater Revenue Bonds | AA+ | AA+ |

The GO bond rating was downgraded from AAA due to issues with state PERA
(Public Employees Retirement Association) pension contributions -- a liability
the city cannot control independently because PERA is a state-administered
system. Scott noted that the city "successfully managed the sale of GO bonds
and earned very good bond ratings" during his tenure.

### Major Capital Initiatives

- **Midtown Campus redevelopment**: A 64-acre mixed-use redevelopment on
  St. Michael's Drive (former College of Santa Fe site). Infrastructure costs
  are estimated at $60-70M. The city is pursuing tax increment financing.
  Housing construction and commercial tenants are now moving in.
- **Utility billing system conversion**: Replacement of the legacy UCIS system,
  described by Scott as "a big deal."
- **Wastewater rate adoption**: New rates were adopted during Scott's tenure;
  water rate revisions are still in progress.
- **IT security and functionality upgrades**: Ongoing modernization.

## Procurement and Operations

### Procurement

Santa Fe's procurement system is modeled on the state of New Mexico's
procurement code. Scott, who served as a city manager for 36 consecutive years
across multiple cities, called it "overly burdensome" and "a big problem." He
noted that he had "never once had a prospective vendor or contractor sue my
city for unfair purchasing practices" in cities with "infinitely simpler
procurement policies."

An internal audit consultant was authorized to evaluate the procurement system
before Scott's departure. He recommended a complete overhaul.

### Grant Management

Scott assessed the city's grant management as "reasonably" effective but lacking
a central clearinghouse. The city recently contracted with an Albuquerque firm
to manage legislative capital grants. Scott recommended expanding and nurturing
that relationship, and establishing centralized oversight for capital project
management more broadly.

### Audit Status

Under Finance Director Emily Oster's leadership, the city's financial audits
were brought current and "document excellence" according to Scott. This was a
significant achievement -- many New Mexico municipalities struggle with audit
backlogs.

## What Makes Santa Fe Different

Santa Fe is not a typical mid-size American city. Several characteristics set it
apart and directly affect how its budget should be interpreted:

### Governance Innovations

- **Ranked-choice voting**: One of a small number of U.S. cities to use RCV in
  municipal elections. The 2025 mayoral race was the city's second RCV cycle.
- **Public campaign financing**: Reduces barriers to running for office and
  appears as a line item in the budget.
- **Direct democracy**: The charter provides for referendum, initiative, recall,
  and a formal citizen complaint process.

### The Capital City Dynamic

The state government's physical presence inside city limits means Santa Fe hosts
significant institutional infrastructure (the Roundhouse, state offices,
courts) without collecting property tax on state-owned land and without
controlling the traffic, parking, and service demands those institutions create.
State employees who commute in contribute to GRT through purchases but may not
live in the city.

### Tourism and Cultural Economy

Santa Fe's identity as a cultural destination is not incidental to its finances
-- it is foundational. Tourism drives lodgers' tax revenue ($18M), supports
GRT through visitor spending, and sustains the arts ecosystem. But tourism
dependence creates seasonal revenue swings and makes the city vulnerable to
external shocks (pandemic, travel trends, economic downturns).

The city's **UNESCO Creative City** designation (2005) was the first in the
United States. Approximately 10% of all employment is in arts and cultural
industries. The budget includes dedicated departments for Arts & Culture
($2.1M) and Tourism Santa Fe ($16.3M).

### Water Scarcity

Water is an existential issue for Santa Fe. The city sits in a high-desert
climate and depends on a complex portfolio of water sources:

- **Buckman Direct Diversion**: Conveys Rio Grande surface water through
  infrastructure partially tunneled under the Continental Divide, supplying
  roughly half the city's water. Jointly operated with Santa Fe County and
  Las Campanas.
- **City wells and reservoirs**: Groundwater and surface water from the Santa Fe
  River watershed.

Water and wastewater operations are the single largest budget item (Public
Utilities at $100.7M). New wastewater rates were recently adopted; water rate
revisions are ongoing.

### Housing Affordability

The gap between home prices and local incomes is among the most severe in the
Mountain West. The city's response includes:

- A dedicated **Affordable Housing department** ($8.6M, up 43% in FY26)
- The **Affordable Housing Trust Fund** with its 3:1 leverage requirement
- A **3% excise tax on home sales over $1M** (upheld 2025)
- An inclusionary zoning ordinance and fee-in-lieu system for developers

### Pension Exposure

City employees participate in the state-administered **PERA** pension system.
PERA's funded status directly affects the city's bond ratings -- the recent GO
bond downgrade from AAA to AA was attributed to PERA contribution issues. The
city has limited ability to influence PERA's investment returns or contribution
schedules, yet bears the financial consequences.

## Reading the Budget Data

When exploring the budget dashboard on this portal, keep in mind:

- **"All Funds" is not all spending.** The $480.6M total excludes the Buckman
  Direct Diversion, the Solid Waste Management Agency, and other entities with
  separate governance.
- **Transfers Out is not discretionary.** The $80.3M in interfund transfers
  reflects money moving between funds, not new spending. But the lack of clear
  transfer policies means the flows are difficult to audit or evaluate.
- **The General Fund treemap shows operating choices.** The $161M General Fund
  is where the governing body has the most discretion. Police, Fire, and Public
  Works consume roughly 63% of it.
- **Revenue growth may not be sustainable.** GRT has grown rapidly but remains
  tied to economic conditions. The narrowing surplus (from $47M to $2M over
  four years) is a structural trend worth watching.
- **Personnel is the dominant cost driver.** At 35% of all spending and growing
  at ~12% annually, personnel costs are the single largest factor in budget
  growth.
- **Year-over-year swings in specific departments may reflect one-time items**
  (capital projects, grant expenditures, insurance settlements) rather than
  changes in ongoing service levels.
